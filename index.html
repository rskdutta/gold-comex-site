This model reads COMEX gold futures through three independent lenses — the physical metal actually changing hands, the dollar it is priced in, and the cost of money. Each tells part of the story; when they agree, the signal is strong. Here is what each is saying now.

01
Physical · COMEX deliveries

Who is actually taking the metal

Every delivery notice names a firm issuing gold (handing real bars out) and one stopping it (standing for and taking them in). Heavy, broad stopping is genuine physical demand; thin or one-name flow is not. The four sub-signals below combine into the CAI.

Latest report · issuers vs stoppers
02
The dollar · DXY

What gold is priced in

Gold is quoted in US dollars, so the dollar’s strength is a mechanical headwind or tailwind. A rising dollar index (DXY) makes gold more expensive abroad and tends to cap it; a falling dollar does the opposite.

03
Macro · 10-Year Treasury (FRED)

What money costs

Gold pays no yield, so the 10-year Treasury rate is its opportunity cost. When real yields rise, holding gold is more expensive and demand softens; when they fall, gold becomes relatively more attractive.
